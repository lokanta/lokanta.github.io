Behind the scenes

Join Bhante Sujato and Ajahn Brahmali, two senior sangha members who have translated the Vinaya and the Sutta Pitaka from pali to English for SuttaCentral. Over fifteen years, SuttaCentral has grown and evolved into the world’s premier platform for reading and studying the Suttas and other early Buddhist scriptures. We have built our own software system for creating translations of Suttas and Vinaya. Join this forum to find out what is happening behind the scenes and how you can help contribute to the next phase of SuttaCentral.

In a Native Garden

A day of spiritual freindship, with a guided tour of Boongala to meet the plants, flowers, bees and the birds, followed by a dhamma discourse and meditation with Bhante Sujato.

Boongala, is an Aboriginal word which means shaded place. The first Boongala garden in NSW was established in the1940s by Sid Cadwell, a native plant pioneer and a legendary figure in the cultivation of Australian plants. When Mal and Jenny started their native garden three decades ago, they named it in the memory Sids Boongala garden.


This is an outdoor event and please bring the following: Sunscreen, hat, water bottle, insect repellent, a picnic blanket, and a cushion. Numbers are limited due to Covid, please register early.

Who are we?

Bhantes Sujato and Akāliko are Australian Buddhist monks, ordained in the Thai lineage of Theravada Buddhism.

  • Bhante Sujato is a senior teaching monk in Australia and the region. He is currently focussing on leading the team for SuttaCentral, a website for early Buddhist texts, translations, and parallels.
  • Bhante Akāliko is a long term student of Bhante Sujato. He took ordination with Ajahn Brahm in Bodhinyana Monastery, Perth. He is the founder of Rainbodhi LGBTQIA+ Buddhist Community. He is a Buddhist chaplain at Western Sydney University and on the Board of Directors of the Buddhist Council of NSW.

At the Monastery at the End of the World we try to be simple, frugal, and content. The little we have is second-hand where possible; either donated or rescued from cast-offs. We rely on the kindness of others, going on alms-round (piṇḍapāta) in our local community for our only meal of the day.

The monks offer an ongoing series of talks and events that will challenge and inspire you to think in new ways about your Dhamma practice.

Almsround and House Dana Invitations

Piṇḍapāta

On weekdays, the monks go walking for alms-food in the local area (piṇḍapāta). Come along and put some food in the monks’ bowls.

Monday to Fridays only. Meet on the corner of Wigram and Marion streets, Harris Park, outside the Post Office around 11:00am.

Almsround is a tradition that dates back to the time of the Buddha. The word piṇḍapāta is a combination of two words piṇḍa and pāta. Piṇḍa means “a lump of food” and pāta means “bowl”. So piṇḍapāta means literally “placing of food in a bowl”.

Piṇḍapāta is not begging. The monks simply walk or stand silently and receive what is offered. It helps to develop contentedness and humility in the monks, and generosity and service for the people offering. Any extra food is shared with the local Community Center on Albion Street, where it is distributed to local people who cannot feed themselves and their family.
